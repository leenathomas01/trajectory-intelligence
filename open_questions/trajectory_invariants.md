# Open Question: Universal Trajectory Invariants

## The Question

Do universal mathematical scaling laws govern trajectory transitions across completely different domains?

A degradation spiral in vascular tissue, a margin erosion pattern in a business unit, and a fatigue accumulation curve in a load-bearing structure share apparent geometric similarity. Is this similarity superficial (analogical) or structural (mathematical)?

## Why It Matters

If trajectory transitions obey universal invariants, then models trained on trajectory data in one domain could transfer to another. A phase transition detector trained on ecological data could, in principle, detect organizational collapse signatures — not because the systems are similar, but because the topology of approach-to-collapse is identical.

If they don't — if each domain requires its own trajectory grammar — then Trajectory Intelligence remains a useful framework but not a unifying theory.

## Existing Evidence

Several fields have independently identified domain-invariant transition signatures:

- Critical Slowing Down appears in ecology, climate science, financial markets, and neuroscience.
- Power-law scaling of cascade sizes appears in earthquakes, blackouts, financial crashes, and epidemic spreads.
- Hysteresis loops appear in materials science, physiology, and market microstructure.

Whether these similarities reflect deep mathematical structure or convergent surface behavior remains unresolved.

## Research Directions

- Comparative analysis of trajectory motifs across domains using a shared embedding space.
- Testing whether trajectory compression codebooks (motif libraries) transfer across domains without retraining.
- Formal mathematical characterization of the conditions under which trajectory dynamics are domain-invariant.
